# EU AI Act, August 2 2026: what high-risk system deployers actually need (and the audit log they probably don't have yet)

**Updated:** 2026-06-09
**For:** CTOs and compliance leads at companies that deploy AI systems into the EU (whether their HQ is in the EU or not).
**TL;DR:** On 2 August 2026, EU AI Act obligations land for high-risk AI system providers and deployers. Articles 12, 14, and 19 require automatic event logging, human-oversight evidence, and ≥6-month retention of those logs. Most agentic-AI deployments today do not produce logs of the shape these articles require. Quill is an open-source MIT artifact that does, and `quill audit export --pack` generates the evidence pack in one command.

---

## What lands on 2 August 2026

The EU AI Act entered into force on 1 August 2024. Obligations have been phasing in since then:

| Date | Obligations effective |
|---|---|
| 2 February 2025 | Prohibited AI practices (Article 5) |
| 2 August 2025 | General-purpose AI obligations (Chapter V) |
| **2 August 2026** | **High-risk AI system obligations, including Articles 12, 14, 19** |
| 2 August 2027 | Embedded high-risk AI in regulated products |

Source: [artificialintelligenceact.eu](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/), the unofficial consolidated reference for the regulation, mirrors the Official Journal text.

The August 2026 set is the one that's been getting the most attention in the last three months because it covers the broadest set of AI deployments. If your AI product touches employment, education, critical infrastructure, credit scoring, law enforcement support, migration / asylum, justice administration, or democratic processes (the eight high-risk areas in Annex III), you're a "deployer" or "provider" under the Act on 2 August.

## Article 12: automatic event logging over the lifetime of the system

Exact statutory language ([Article 12 official text](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/article/12/)):

> *"High-risk AI systems shall technically allow for the automatic recording of events (logs) over the lifetime of the system."*

The logs must support three purposes:
- (a) identifying risk situations or substantial modifications, including triggering the obligations in Article 79(1)
- (b) facilitating post-market monitoring (Article 72)
- (c) monitoring operation per Article 26(5)

For biometric identification systems specifically, Article 12 names the minimum fields: start/end timestamps of each use, reference database checked, input data that matched, identification of natural persons verifying results. For non-biometric high-risk systems, the field set is left to the provider but must satisfy the three purposes above.

**What this means in practice:** if your AI agent dispatches tool calls (Bash, Edit, deploy, API calls, MCP server invocations), every dispatch is an "event" that the deployment "automatically generated." Article 12 requires you to record it.

## Article 19: minimum 6-month log retention

Exact statutory language ([Article 19 official text](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/article/19/)):

> *"Providers of high-risk AI systems shall keep the logs referred to in Article 12(1), automatically generated by their high-risk AI systems, to the extent such logs are under their control. (...) The logs shall be kept for a period appropriate to the intended purpose of the high-risk AI system, of at least six months, unless provided otherwise in the applicable Union or national law, in particular in Union law on the protection of personal data."*

Three things worth pulling out:
1. **Six months is the floor.** Biometric and law-enforcement systems often require 24+ months per Article 26(6); GDPR can extend further if the logs contain personal data.
2. **Provider-side obligation.** Article 19 is primarily about providers. Deployers also have logging obligations under Article 26(6), and those obligations can extend retention substantially.
3. **"To the extent under their control."** If you're a deployer of someone else's high-risk AI system, you're not on the hook for retention of logs the provider holds. But the moment your deployment produces logs (which any tool-using agent does), those logs are under your control.

## Article 14: human oversight

Exact statutory language ([Article 14 official text](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/article/14/)):

> *"High-risk AI systems shall be designed and developed in such a way, including with appropriate human-machine interface tools, that they can be effectively overseen by natural persons during the period in which they are in use."*

Paragraph 4 specifies what "effectively overseen" means — deployers must be able to:
- Understand the relevant capacities and limitations of the high-risk AI system
- Remain aware of "automation bias" (the tendency to over-rely on system outputs)
- Correctly interpret the AI system's output
- Decide, in any particular situation, not to use the high-risk AI system
- Intervene or interrupt the system through a "stop" button or similar
- Where appropriate, override its output

Three capability levels in the statute: understand, intervene, halt. The "human-in-the-loop / on-the-loop / in-command" taxonomy that gets quoted in commentary is *not* in the statute itself (it's a Maarten-Stolk-era commentary framework). The statute language is what's binding.

**What this means in practice:** for an AI coding agent that dispatches `git push --force`, `DROP TABLE`, `vercel --prod`, or any other destructive operation, the deployer must be able to demonstrate that a natural person had the opportunity to intervene before the operation executed. The evidence is the audit log entry showing the operator's decision (allow / deny / paused-for-confirmation) with a timestamp and a reason.

## What most deployments don't have today

Three gaps I see consistently in pre-August conversations with founders:

**Gap 1: agent tool calls don't produce logs.** Most coding-agent integrations (Claude Code, Cursor, Cline, Aider, Continue, Windsurf) log to the terminal or to the IDE's UI. There's no persistent, tamper-evident, structured log of every tool call. Article 12 requires automatic logging. Telemetry sent to a vendor isn't enough; the deployer needs their own copy of the log.

**Gap 2: there's no tamper-evidence on the logs that do exist.** A plaintext log file can be edited after the fact, including by the agent itself if the agent has filesystem write access. Auditor expectation for "tamper-evidence" includes append-only storage, cryptographic chaining, and immutable records. ISO 42001 control A.6.2.8 explicitly names these criteria; the EU AI Act doesn't quote them, but Article 12(b)'s post-market monitoring purpose implies them (you can't monitor a log that's been edited).

**Gap 3: human-oversight evidence is the weakest link.** Even deployments that log tool calls rarely log the human's approval decision. The Article 14 evidence shape is: *who approved this, when, with what reasoning, and what changed because of it?* Most current setups answer two of those four fields at best.

## How Quill addresses each article

[Quill](https://github.com/manumarri-sudo/quill) is an open-source MIT artifact that gates AI agent tool calls before they execute and records every gate decision into an HMAC-SHA256-chained JSONL audit log. The format is specifically designed to satisfy Articles 12, 14, and 19.

| Article | Requirement | Quill's evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Art. 12(1)** | Automatic recording of events over the system's lifetime | `tool.attempted`, `verdict.allowed`, `verdict.blocked`, `verdict.ask` events emitted before every gated tool dispatch |
| **Art. 12(1)(a)** | Identifying risk situations / substantial modifications | `session.taint.update`, `tool.pin_refused`, `chain.repaired` events surface the categories Article 79(1) cares about |
| **Art. 12 implied** | Tamper-evidence | HMAC-SHA256 chain over canonical event payloads; `fcntl.flock` cross-process serialization; verifiable with `quill audit verify` |
| **Art. 14(4)(a)** | Operators understand the system | Session intent + scope declared at `session.open`; reason field on every verdict |
| **Art. 14(4)(d)** | Operators decide not to use | `verdict.ask` event + the type-to-confirm flow + Touch ID + paste-able one-shot tokens |
| **Art. 14(4)(e)** | Operators intervene or interrupt | `verdict.blocked` with reason; `approve.biometric.deny` records the operator's refusal |
| **Art. 14(4)(f)** | Operators override system output | `approve.biometric.ok` plus the one-shot `quill approve <token>` audit trail |
| **Art. 19** | ≥6-month retention | Append-only JSONL on the deployer's own disk, mode `0o600`; the operator owns the retention discipline (default location `$QUILL_HOME/audit.log.jsonl`, configurable per project) |

The full per-control crosswalk lives in [`src/quill/controls.toml`](../../src/quill/controls.toml) and drives the `quill audit export --pack` deliverable.

## The honest read on what this doesn't fix

- **Quill is not AIUC-1 certification.** AIUC-1 is granted by an accredited auditor (Schellman is currently the only one) on the full management system. Quill is one evidence source. The operator still owns model evals, red-team results, data lineage, and the rest of the AIUC-1 control set.
- **Quill is not a substitute for a legal review.** Whether your specific deployment falls under Annex III "high-risk" classification is a legal question that depends on the deployment context. Quill assumes you've already concluded that you need Article 12 + 14 + 19 evidence; if you haven't, talk to your privacy counsel before assuming the answer.
- **Quill is not the only thing you need.** Article 14 oversight requires more than logs; it requires UX, documentation, training, and an organizational pattern. Quill is the logging substrate that makes the rest credible.

## How to get started before August 2

If you're a CTO with an EU customer and the August 2 deadline is on your P0 list:

**Week 1**: install Quill on every engineer's laptop, configure your shared notification channel (Slack webhook is the most common). One command per laptop: `uvx quillx start` or `pipx install quillx && quill onboard`. Verify the audit chain populates: `quill audit show --last 10`.

**Week 2**: run Quill against your CI / agent pipelines (`quill serve` as an MCP proxy in front of your agent's tool surface). Confirm the audit log captures pipeline events the same way it captures developer-laptop events.

**Week 3**: produce a sample evidence pack via `quill audit export --pack` and hand it to your auditor for a dry run. The auditor's first pass surfaces gaps; address them before August 2.

**Week 4**: write your Article 14 oversight documentation, with the Quill log as the runtime substrate. Pair it with whatever model-eval and red-team artifacts your AIUC-1 / ISO 42001 process requires.

**On August 2**: continue as before. The deadline is a threshold, not a project. Your log persists, your auditor samples on a quarterly cadence (per AIUC-1's published rhythm), and the evidence pack regenerates from `quill audit export --pack` whenever you need a refreshed view.
